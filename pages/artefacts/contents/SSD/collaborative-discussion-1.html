The Cross-Site-Scripting (XSS) attack is a common technique to inject malicious JavaScript into the victim’s browser and execute suspicious code [1]. The below figure dictate the flow of how the XSS attack works. For example, attackers will ask the victim to log in to their bank account. After the victim gets authorised from the bank, attackers can ascertain sensitive information from the end user’s browser, such as tokens, sessions, and cookies. So, attackers can disguise to make a valid transfer request to the bank with the client’s authorised session.


Figure 1 – The flow of the Cross-Site-Scripting attack

The OWASP foundation (N.D.) indicated that we should never trust the information sent from the client side with no validation on those data [2]. It also provides various recommendations to mitigate the impact of the XSS attack. We focus on the rule “Use HTTPOnly cookie flag” for the above case. When setting cookies with a specified “HTTPOnly” flag, the client-side script will get an empty string instead of the actual value from cookies [3]. As a result, the XSS attack will fail to ascertain the sensitive information from the cookies.
